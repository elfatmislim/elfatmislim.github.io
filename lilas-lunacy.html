Brand Failure

The Lilas Lunacy: A Behavioural Economics Dissection of Tunisia's Most Expensive Marketing Misadventure

Slim El Fatmi· July 21, 2025

I have a theory about marketing catastrophes: they're infinitely more instructive than marketing successes. Success can be attributed to luck, timing, or superior products—but spectacular failure requires a perfect storm of psychological misunderstanding, strategic delusion, and behavioral blindness that illuminates exactly how human minds actually work.

Which brings me to the Lilas cosmetics debacle of 2024—a case study so rich in cognitive errors and psychological miscalculations that it deserves its own wing in the Museum of Marketing Malpractice. Here we have a brand that spent decades mastering the dark arts of household product positioning, only to discover that expertise in toilet paper manufacturing doesn't automatically translate to beauty industry credibility.

The Alchemy of Brand Meaning

Lilas had spent decades doing something rather magical: they'd transmuted mundane household products into genuine brand equity. In the mysterious alchemy of consumer psychology, they'd become the "smart local choice." This is no small achievement. Creating brand preference in commodity categories requires a peculiar kind of genius. You're essentially convincing people that your sodium lauryl sulfate is somehow more desirable than everyone else's sodium lauryl sulfate. Lilas had cracked this code through decades of consistent quality, competitive pricing, and smart positioning as the patriotic alternative to foreign brands.

And then, in 2024, someone decided this wasn't enough.

The Academic Foundation of Brand Extension Failure

Before we dive deeper into the psychological mechanics of this disaster, it's worth examining the academic research that predicted exactly this outcome. David Aaker and Kevin Lane Keller's seminal 1990 study on brand extensions provides the theoretical framework that makes Lilas's failure so predictable — and so instructive.

Their research identified two critical variables determining brand extension success. First: perceived quality transfer — does the brand's quality reputation translate to the new category? Second: perceived fit — does the extension make sense given what consumers know about the brand?

For Lilas, both variables failed catastrophically.

The Perceived Quality Transfer Problem

Lilas is brilliant at cleaning things. Their products remove stains, eliminate germs, maintain hygiene. This is their core competence, and consumers know it. But here's the cognitive trap: "good at cleaning" does not translate to "good at beautifying."

In the consumer's mental model, cleaning and beautifying are not just different — they're almost opposite activities. Cleaning removes things. Beauty adds things. Cleaning standardizes. Beauty individualizes. Cleaning is about elimination. Beauty is about transformation.

When Lilas launched beauty products, consumers faced a fundamental cognitive dissonance: "The brand that makes my bathroom spotless wants to put something on my face?"

The Perceived Fit Catastrophe

This is where the brand association problem becomes terminal. A brand built on household cleanliness carries implicit associations with the elimination of dirt, germs, and contamination. These associations are powerful. They're deeply embedded through years of marketing and product experience.

But these associations are also deeply incompatible with beauty.

Think about what beauty products are supposed to evoke: sensuality, transformation, aspiration, femininity, luxury. Now think about the associations Lilas has spent decades building: cleanliness, practicality, hygiene, domestic reliability.

These aren't just different. They're almost antithetical.

When you've trained consumers to associate your brand with toilet bowl cleaning and floor mopping, launching a mascara requires them to perform a mental gymnastic feat that most won't bother with.

The Category Entry Points Problem

Beyond the brand association problem, Lilas faced a structural challenge in the beauty category that they appear not to have understood: the purchase journey for beauty products is fundamentally different from household cleaning products.

Household cleaning products are purchased on routine and necessity. You run out of floor cleaner, you buy floor cleaner. The decision process involves minimal emotional engagement.

Beauty products involve trial, recommendation, aspiration, and ongoing relationship. Consumers try beauty products with friends. They ask for recommendations from people they trust. They form emotional attachments to products that "work for them." The purchase process is entirely different.

Lilas entered a category requiring investment in consumer relationships and brand experience with a strategy apparently designed for commodity procurement. That's not just a brand mistake. That's a category misunderstanding.

The Retailer Trust Transfer Failure

There's another dimension of this failure that deserves analysis: retail placement and context effects.

When Lilas beauty products appeared on shelves, where did they appear? In the cleaning products aisle of supermarkets — right next to their household cleaning range. This placement was logical from a distribution standpoint (same retail relationships, same shelf space negotiations) but psychologically catastrophic.

Seeing a mascara next to a toilet bowl cleaner creates an irresolvable category confusion. It's not just that the product seems incongruous — it's that the placement actively contaminates the beauty product with cleaning product associations. Context shapes meaning, and the context Lilas chose was as unfavorable as possible.

What Lilas Should Have Done

If the strategic goal was diversification into personal care, there were paths that might have worked. They involved either:

Creating a completely separate brand (most expensive but psychologically correct) — launch the beauty range under a different brand name with no visible Lilas connection. Build this brand from scratch with beauty-appropriate associations.

Entering adjacent personal care categories with more overlap (body wash, hand cream) where "clean and hygienic" is an asset rather than a liability. Build from there over many years.

Acquiring an established beauty brand with existing associations and consumer trust, then leveraging Lilas's distribution rather than its brand.

Instead, they chose the worst possible option: taking the Lilas brand directly into high-involvement beauty categories where their existing associations were actively harmful.

The Broader Lesson

The Lilas debacle teaches us something fundamental about brand strategy that applies far beyond this specific case.

Brand equity is not a generic asset. It's category-specific. Lilas has enormous brand equity — but that equity is specifically relevant in household cleaning. It cannot be transferred wholesale to categories with different psychological requirements.

Brand extension success requires two things to be true simultaneously: consumers must believe the brand can deliver quality in the new category, AND the brand's associations must be compatible with the new category's psychological requirements.

For most extensions, one of these conditions fails. For Lilas beauty products, both conditions failed — comprehensively.

Marketing is ultimately the management of associations in consumers' minds. Build the wrong associations for a category — or try to transplant the right associations from the wrong context — and no amount of advertising budget can rescue you.

Lilas learned this lesson expensively. You don't have to.

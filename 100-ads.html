I Watched 100 Tunisian Commercials and What I Found Will Surprise You

Slim El Fatmi · January 21, 2026 · 14 min read

I have a theory about Tunisian television advertising, and like all properly interesting theories, it came to me sideways, through a circuitous route involving pregnant women, Finnish birds, and Renaissance Florence. For the longest time, I watched Tunisian ads with a nagging sense that something was off. I knew these ads lacked tension and clarity, but I felt — and this is the crucial bit — that it was more than that.

Then I stumbled upon Orlando Wood's work, and suddenly the fog lifted in that delightful way that only happens when someone gives you the conceptual vocabulary you've been groping for in the dark.

The Bird Who Knew Too Much

Orlando Wood's eureka moment came from Iain McGilchrist's work on brain hemispheres. Consider the Finnish bird. The left brain, controlling the right eye, is constantly scanning the ground in a narrow, goal-oriented fashion, looking for grains. Meanwhile, the right brain is looking around in broad, vigilant sweeps, making absolutely certain our feathered friend doesn't become someone else's lunch. Two conflicting modes of attention. And it's not just birds — it's us.

The Tale of Two Hemispheres

The left brain is narrow, goal-oriented, abstract, categorical. It adores cause-and-effect, repeatability, rhythm, language, signs, symbols. It's literal. The right brain is broad and vigilant. It understands context, sees wholes rather than parts. It reads people — their body language, gestures, intonations. It's open to novelty, which means it understands metaphor, humor, and art.

What 100 Tunisian Ads Reveal

I built a scoring system and analyzed 100 Tunisian TV commercials. I identified 10 right-brain features and 10 left-brain features from Wood's research. Each ad gets a point for every feature present, resulting in a Left–Right Brain Index (LRBI) = RBS − LBS, ranging from −10 (pure medieval control) to +10 (pure Renaissance narrative).

Across the entire corpus, 72–78% of ads are left-brain dominant. Only 5–8% can be classified as right-brain dominant. If Tunisian TV advertising were a democracy, the right brain would have lost its deposit.

FMCG & Telecom average LRBI: −5 to −7. Automotive: +3 to +6. Tata Motors 2025 scored LRBI of +6 — practically a short film that happens to feature a vehicle. Kaiser 2021 scored LRBI of −8 — less an advertisement than a mnemonic device disguised as thirty seconds of television.

The Five Archetypes of Tunisian Advertising

1. The Flat Rhythmic Musical Comedy — Bright colors, bouncy music, people doing a sort of vague choreography that suggests joy without quite committing to actual dancing. Advertising as jingle delivery system. Average LRBI: −6.

2. The Flat Descriptive Bad Poetry — Product attributes delivered in tortured rhyming couplets over slow-motion footage. A brand strategy sheet mugged by a rhyming dictionary. Average LRBI: −5.

3. The Problem-Solution Narrative While Singing — A peculiarly Tunisian innovation. Someone has a problem and rather than acting to solve it, they sing their way through the entire narrative arc. Average LRBI: −4.

4. The Explanatory Musical Monologue — A person simply explains the situation while singing. Advertising as sung PowerPoint presentation. Average LRBI: −7.

5. The Nostalgia Reference — The refuge of the creatively exhausted. Someone remembers childhood, always in soft focus, always with a knowing wink. Average LRBI: −3.

The Medieval Present

We've created the advertising equivalent of medieval painting — flat, abstract, obsessed with control. We have cinematographers who could shoot the next great Tunisian film, and we're having them point cameras at people singing about dairy products.

When everything is shouting, a whisper becomes deafening. Silence, place, and progression are the biggest untapped creative assets in Tunisian advertising.

The Magnificent Middle: Tunisia's Accidental Revolution

Slim El Fatmi · February 7, 2026 ·
10 min read
Key Insights
  • The middle market in Tunisia is not a compromise. It is the dominant consumer reality and the fastest-growing segment.
  • The Goldilocks Lock: the middle only wins when premium is psychologically absurd, budget fails functionally, and the middle product actually does the job. All three conditions simultaneously.
  • Peak Sports, El Fawe7, and Infinix did not just find a price gap. They found a performance vacuum created by simultaneous failure at both extremes.

The brands winning the Tunisian market are not the ones with the best story. They are the ones with the most consistently adequate execution. This is not a consolation prize. This is a competitive moat.

I have stumbled upon something rather peculiar in Tunisia. Not a revolution in the traditional sense, no barricades, no manifestos, but a quiet commercial insurgency that is rewriting the rules of how markets actually work when theory collides with the messy reality of human psychology and relentless inflation.

What I am watching is this: a complete inversion of conventional marketing wisdom, where the brands winning1 are not the premium players or the budget disruptors, but something altogether more interesting: the adequate middle. The "good enough" brands. The ones that business school professors would mark down for poor positioning. And they are absolutely printing money2.

When Premium Becomes Preposterous

Inflation has not just made things expensive. It has broken the fundamental psychological contract between premium brands and their customers. When a pair of Nike trainers costs what feels like a month's salary, something rather important happens in the consumer's mind. It is not that they cannot afford it. It is that the transaction stops making psychological sense.

Luxury works when there is surplus value, when paying more delivers a feeling of gain, of winning, of getting something extra. But when inflation pushes prices into the realm of the absurd, that surplus evaporates. You are not buying status anymore. You are buying a creeping sense that you are being taken for a fool. The economic term is "consumer surplus." The human term is "do I feel like an idiot for buying this?"

The Bottom Is Not Much Better

So people flee downmarket. Perfectly rational, you would think. Except the bottom of the market presents its own form of stupidity. The cheap trainers fall apart. The knock-off perfume smells like regret. The budget smartphone makes you want to throw it against a wall.

This creates what I am going to call The Stupid Squeeze: premium makes you feel financially stupid, budget makes you feel functionally stupid. Now, this is where it gets interesting.

The Two Sacred Conditions

The middle only wins when two very specific conditions exist simultaneously. Like a perfect storm, or a soufflé: get the timing wrong and the whole thing collapses.

Condition One: The Market Extremes Must Both Fail. Part A: Premium must be absurdly out of touch. Not just expensive. Premium must have crossed into the realm where it creates negative consumer surplus. The price is so divorced from reality that buying it does not make you feel successful, it makes you feel like you have been mugged by a logo. Part B: Low-cost must fail to do the job. Not "it's not quite as good." The cheap option must be so inadequate that it does not fulfill the basic function you are hiring it to perform.

Condition Two: Your Middle Product Must Actually Do The Job. Not better than premium. Not as fancy. Just competent. Adequate. Functional. You are clearing a threshold, the threshold of "does this actually work?"

Both conditions. Not one. Both.

The Perfect Storm in Action

Peak Sports: Nike and Adidas had become genuinely absurd, prices so high they had left the atmosphere entirely. Cheap alternatives fell apart. Peak Sports made trainers that actually worked. Comfortable. Durable enough. Did the job of being footwear. Result: they own the market.

El Fawe7: Premium perfumes at prices that make you think you are funding a small nation. Cheap perfumes smelled chemical and lasted eleven minutes. El Fawe7 at 40 dinars created fragrances that actually did what perfume is supposed to do. Result: phenomenal success.

Infinix: Samsung flagships priced in the stratosphere. Truly cheap phones crashed constantly and could not run apps. Infinix made phones that actually functioned as phones. Result: market dominance.

These brands did not just identify a price gap. They identified a performance vacuum created by simultaneous failure at both extremes.

The Sunglasses Opportunity

Which brings us to sunglasses, a category where all three conditions are blinking in neon. Designer sunglasses at 500-700 dinars in Tunisia's inflationary environment: no consumer surplus, just anxiety about spending half a month's salary on something you might sit on. Budget sunglasses: poor UV protection, distorted lenses that give you headaches, frames that snap, and they make you look ridiculous.

The opportunity is to create sunglasses that actually work on all dimensions: proper UV protection, decent optical quality, frames that survive normal use, and designs that make you look presentable. At 50-80 dinars instead of 500-700. Because sunglasses sit on your face all day. The job is not just optical protection. It is social protection too. Budget sunglasses fail at both. The three conditions are perfectly aligned.

The Broader Pattern

What is happening in Tunisia is not unique. It is just more visible because inflation has created a perfect laboratory. The successful brands have not stumbled into success. They have identified markets where premium has become absurd, budget does not work, and they can deliver adequacy at a sensible price. All three conditions. Simultaneously.

You are not offering the best or the cheapest. You are offering people the ability to make a defensible decision, to feel clever instead of foolish, to get the job done without bankrupting themselves or buying rubbish. That is worth considerably more than being merely cheap or merely premium.

This is not about being "good enough." It is about being the only sensible option in a market of stupid extremes. Which does not fit on a business card, admittedly. But it does fit rather nicely in a bank account.

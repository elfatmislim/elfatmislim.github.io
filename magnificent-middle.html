The Magnificent Middle: Tunisia's Accidental Revolution

Slim El Fatmi · February 7, 2026 ·
10 min read
Key Insights
  • The middle market in Tunisia is not a compromise — it's the dominant consumer reality.
  • Brands chasing premium positioning are ignoring the segment that's actually growing.
  • Good enough, consistently delivered, at the right price is a genuine competitive strategy.

I've stumbled upon something rather peculiar in Tunisia. Not a revolution in the traditional sense — no barricades, no manifestos — but a quiet commercial insurgency that's rewriting the rules of how markets actually work when theory collides with the messy reality of human psychology and relentless inflation.

What I'm watching is this: a complete inversion of conventional marketing wisdom, where the brands winning aren't the premium players3 or the budget disruptors, but something altogether more interesting — the adequate middle1. The "good enough" brands. The ones that business school professors would mark down for poor positioning. And they're absolutely printing money2.

When Premium Becomes Preposterous

Inflation hasn't just made things expensive — it's broken the fundamental psychological contract between premium brands and their customers. When a pair of Nike trainers costs what feels like a month's salary, the transaction stops making psychological sense. You're not buying status anymore; you're buying a creeping sense that you're being taken for a fool. The economic term is "consumer surplus." The human term is: "do I feel like an idiot for buying this?"

So people flee downmarket. Except the bottom of the market presents its own form of stupidity. The cheap trainers fall apart. The knock-off perfume smells like regret. This creates what I'm calling The Stupid Squeeze: premium makes you feel financially stupid, budget makes you feel functionally stupid.

The Two Sacred Conditions

The middle only wins when two very specific conditions exist simultaneously. Condition One: the market extremes must both fail — premium must be psychologically absurd, AND budget must fail to do the actual job. Condition Two: your middle product must actually do the job. Not better than premium. Not as fancy. Just competent. Adequate. Functional. Both conditions. Not one. Both.

The Perfect Storm in Action

Peak Sports: Nike and Adidas had become genuinely absurd in price. Cheap alternatives hurt your feet and fell apart. Peak Sports made trainers that actually worked. Result? They own the market.

El Fawe7: Premium perfumes had become ludicrously expensive. Cheap perfumes lasted eleven minutes. El Fawe7 at 40 dinars created fragrances that actually did what perfume is supposed to do. Result? Phenomenal success.

Infinix: Samsung flagships priced in the stratosphere. Truly cheap phones couldn't run apps properly. Infinix made phones that functioned as phones. Result? Market dominance.

These brands didn't just identify a price gap. They identified a performance vacuum created by simultaneous failure at both extremes.

The Sunglasses Opportunity

Which brings us to sunglasses — a category where all three conditions are blinking in neon. Designer sunglasses at 500–700 dinars for what costs 30 dinars to manufacture. Budget sunglasses fail at multiple jobs simultaneously: poor UV protection, distorted lenses, frames that snap. The opportunity: sunglasses that actually work at 50–80 dinars. It's almost unfair how obvious the opportunity is.

The successful brands haven't stumbled into success. They've identified markets where premium has become absurd, budget doesn't work, and they can deliver adequacy at a sensible price. You're not offering the best or the cheapest. You're offering people the ability to make a defensible decision — to feel clever instead of foolish. And that fits rather nicely in a bank account.

Designing the Mousetrap: How Aziza Wins Without Saying a Word

Slim El Fatmi· July 14, 2025

In the bustling neighborhoods of Tunisia, a retail phenomenon has quietly emerged that demonstrates the power of behavioral psychology in action. Aziza, a local supermarket chain targeting family caregivers and household managers, has created one of the most sophisticated applications of habit-forming psychology I've encountered in retail — whether by design or happy accident.

Exploiting Social Isolation

Walk into any Aziza location and you'll witness something remarkable: customers know the cashiers by name. They exchange pleasantries about family, discuss local events, and genuinely seem to care about each other's wellbeing. What started as transactional interactions have evolved into authentic friendships. Regular customers often coordinate their shopping trips, turning routine errands into informal social gatherings right in the aisles. Aziza didn't explicitly position themselves as the solution to social isolation, but their stores have naturally evolved into what sociologists call the 'third place.'

The Variable Reward: Engineering Uncertainty

Every time a customer enters an Aziza supermarket, they encounter promotions — but not predictable ones. The key isn't the reward itself; it's the variability and consistency of the reward system.

This is the same psychological principle that makes slot machines so addictive and keeps us scrolling through social media feeds. Sometimes it's a major discount on essential items they desperately need. Other times, it's a modest promotion on something less relevant. The variability transforms a routine grocery run into something with an element of discovery. Will today be the day I find something amazing? That question brings people back.

What Aziza has built — knowingly or not — is a complete behavioral loop: social belonging that creates emotional investment, variable rewards that create anticipation, and consistent quality that creates trust. Each visit reinforces the next. The mousetrap doesn't need a sign. The customers just keep coming back.

The Honey Seller Who Understood Psychology Better Than Most Marketing Directors

Slim El Fatmi· August 21, 2025

Sometimes the most interesting business stories come from people who've never read a marketing textbook. They just watch their customers carefully and trust their instincts about human nature. Meet Hazem Boumiza, who took over his family's honey business in Tunisia and figured out what works by paying attention to how people actually behave.

The Problem With Being Too Logical

If you were a rational business consultant looking at Tunisia's honey market, you'd probably suggest selling directly from farms or getting into supermarkets. Both seem perfectly logical. Both would have failed spectacularly. Here's the thing about honey: people don't think of it as a product. They think of it as nature itself, bottled. The very idea of honey sitting on a supermarket shelf next to processed foods feels fundamentally wrong. It's like selling sunset in a can.

The Beautiful Solution

Hazem found what I like to call the 'Goldilocks position' — not too industrial, not too inconvenient, but just right. He set up honey stands in shopping malls. You're already at the mall, so there's no extra effort required. But you're not in a supermarket, so the honey feels special, artisanal, authentic. It's positioned between the boring and the inconvenient. This works perfectly with BJ Fogg's B=MAP model — Behavior equals Motivation times Ability times Prompt.

The Goldilocks position: not too industrial, not too inconvenient. Positioned between the boring and the inconvenient — exactly where trust and convenience meet.

What makes Hazem's story remarkable isn't just the location strategy. It's the entire system: the visual merchandising that communicates artisanal quality, the tastings that convert browsers into believers, the pricing that signals premium without intimidating, and the packaging that looks like a gift rather than a commodity purchase. None of this was in a textbook. All of it was in human psychology.

Who I Am

I am a marketing strategist and independent consultant based in Tunis. My formation is in management — IHEC Carthage — but my real education happened elsewhere: in cognitive psychology, behavioral economics, and neuroscience. Three disciplines that turned out to be more useful for understanding brands than any marketing textbook I ever read.

I am constitutionally incapable of looking at a market without trying to find its underlying logic. Not the surface patterns — the taglines, the campaigns, the press releases — but the deeper architecture: what the brand is actually promising, what the consumer actually believes, and why the gap between the two is almost always wider than anyone in the room wants to admit.

The most interesting thing about Tunisian marketing is not what brands do. It is what they systematically refuse to understand about the people they are supposedly trying to reach.

TIMN — The Tunisian Institute for Marketing Nonsense — started as a private frustration and became a public project. The frustration was simple: nobody was applying rigorous analytical frameworks to Tunisian brands. Not seriously. Not with the same intellectual standards that get applied to Western markets. I decided to do it myself, with 20 articles and counting, covering everything from tuna cans to luxury brand extensions gone catastrophically wrong.

Why I Write

I write because pattern recognition is an obsession I cannot turn off. Every market is a system. Every campaign is a hypothesis. Every brand decision is a claim about human psychology — and most of those claims are either untested, wrong, or both. Writing forces me to make the patterns explicit rather than just intuiting them and moving on.

I also write because the Tunisian market is genuinely underanalyzed. Brands that would generate serious case studies in London or Paris get approximately zero rigorous attention here. That gap is an opportunity. And frankly, watching a brand make an entirely preventable mistake while nobody names the mistake is the kind of thing that keeps me up at night.

Who I Read

These are the thinkers whose work I return to repeatedly — not because they agree with each other, but because each one changed something fundamental about how I see markets and minds.

Mark Ritson The clearest thinker on marketing strategy alive. His willingness to say the obvious thing that everyone is too polite to say is a professional model.
Les Binet & Peter Field The IPA data that finally proved what long-term brand building actually does. Required reading before anyone touches an ad budget.
Orlando Wood The Left-Right Brain Index changed how I watch advertising. I owe the entire LRBI article — and the 100-ads study — to his framework.
Rory Sutherland The man who made irrationality respectable. His argument that psychological value is as real as functional value is the foundation of half my analyses.
John Vervaeke Cognitive science and the nature of relevance. Understanding how the mind assigns meaning to signals reshaped how I think about brand salience.
Daniel Kahneman Thinking, Fast and Slow is the single book that explains the most about why consumers behave differently from how brands expect them to.

"The market doesn't reward good intentions. It rewards accurate models of how people actually behave — and punishes everyone who substitutes wishful thinking for that."

What I Do

Beyond writing, I work as an independent marketing consultant. My focus is strategic analysis — brand positioning, advertising effectiveness audits, and behavioral economics applied to specific market contexts. I don't build campaigns. I diagnose the thinking behind them.

If you are a brand or a team trying to understand why your strategy isn't producing the results you expected, or trying to build something in the Tunisian market with a clearer understanding of what actually drives consumer behavior here — that is the work I do.

The best way to reach me is LinkedIn. I respond to people who have read at least one article and have a specific question. I do not respond to requests for free advice packaged as "a quick chat."

Formation

Management — IHEC Carthage, Tunisia's leading business school. Supplemented by an ongoing autodidactic program in cognitive science, behavioral economics, and advertising effectiveness research that has cost significantly more in time than the degree did.

What TIMN is

The Tunisian Institute for Marketing Nonsense is a one-person research project. The name is deliberately ironic — the nonsense is in the market, not in the analysis. 20 articles. 18 brands. 100 ads. One market that deserves more rigorous attention than it gets.

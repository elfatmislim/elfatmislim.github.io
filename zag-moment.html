Tunisia's Zag Moment: Why Quality Is the New Disruption

Slim El Fatmi · January 17, 2026 · 11 min read

There's a rather depressing game you can play in any Tunisian supermarket. Pick up a 2-liter water bottle. No, really — try to pick it up with one hand. You can't, can you? The plastic has become so gossamer-thin that the bottle crumples like a defeated soufflé the moment you apply the slightest pressure. It's not a water bottle anymore; it's a water suggestion.

Walk down the chocolate aisle and you'll find the same story, told in diminishing weights and expanding air pockets. Tris, Maestro, Gaucho — they've all joined the Great Thinning. Each year, the bars get lighter, airier, more insubstantial. Pick one up and you're holding what feels less like chocolate and more like the memory of chocolate. And it's not just wasteful — it's catastrophically stupid.

The Misconception: A Tragic Oversimplification

Here's where most product managers get it spectacularly wrong: they think of "product" as a discrete, quantifiable thing that exists independently of human perception. Change the configuration slightly, shave off 5 grams, pump in some air, and — your uncle — same product, better margins. Except it's not the same product at all. Product is subordinate to experience, and experience is the most important piece.

The perceptual variables that matter: resistance gradients (how a bottle feels when squeezed), mouthfeel complexity, fracture pattern (how chocolate snaps), dissolution dynamics. These aren't marginal details. They're the entire game. Mess with one element and everything falls apart. It's like removing a minor chord from a symphony.

The Risk Epidemic

Tunisian companies suffer from catastrophically low risk tolerance. If you're a product developer, you must quantify your ideas to get validation from management. But innovation is fundamentally unquantifiable, while cost-cutting offers the siren song of certainty: cut X grams of material, save Y dinars. Guaranteed. They've become addicted to it like rats pressing a lever for dopamine hits.

The product developer who says "I think if we use proper tempering and real cocoa butter, people will love it and tell their friends" gets laughed out of the room. Where are your numbers?

The Category Killer Hypothesis

When every chocolate bar in Tunisia degrades simultaneously, consumers don't just switch brands. They might switch categories entirely. Your brain has a prototype for "chocolate bar" — an idealized average built from years of experience. When products degrade below that prototype, they stop feeling like the real thing. You've exited the category in consumers' minds, and all the marketing in the world won't get you back in.

The Opportunity: Contrast Volatility

Product quality has become so uniformly, so catastrophically terrible that the simple act of making a good product will make you instantly viral. Not "might make you viral." Will make you viral. When everything around you is disappointingly light and vaguely unsatisfying, encountering something with actual substance is a physical shock to the system. It's like walking through a desert and finding a waterfall.

Look at Waffle Chocotom in Tunisia. They didn't hire a fancy agency. They simply made a waffle that actually felt substantial — a waffle with weight, with presence. And people couldn't stop talking about it. Because in a market where every waffle feels like eating sweetened air, encountering one with actual heft is remarkable. Literally: worth remarking upon.

This is Contrast Volatility: in a degraded landscape, basic competence becomes newsworthy. Quality becomes its own marketing department.

The Way Forward

Make a water bottle people can actually hold. Make chocolate that snaps properly and melts luxuriously. Make products that respect the remarkable perceptual machinery your customers are walking around with. The contrast will be so dramatic that you won't need much marketing at all. Quality will be its own advertisement. But it requires courage. It requires believing that the unquantifiable aspects of human experience matter more than the tidy numbers in your spreadsheet.

In the end, the question isn't whether you can afford to make a better product. It's whether you can afford not to.
